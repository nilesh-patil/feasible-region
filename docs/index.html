Cold open

In 1939, George Stigler set out to find the cheapest diet that could keep a person alive and well: seventy-seven foods, no computer. His team ground through the arithmetic by hand, roughly 120 person-days of it, and landed on $39.93 a year. It was very nearly right. The method that could prove it would not arrive for another decade.

Ten thousand years later, that same mathematics has become plumbing. The General Systems Vehicle Marginal Utility provisions itself every few milliseconds: what to fabricate, what to spend, what to keep in reserve, against limits that shift by the second. It settles Stigler's question, and harder ones, before the thought finishes. Here is how it does that.

Section 01 · The hero figure

A region with corners

Deep in the Marginal Utility, a fabricator bay splits its shift between field-tile and shear-foam. Two budgets bind it, feedstock and press time; neither can be crossed. The Mind wants the most valuable mix it can pour.

Underneath, two variables: maximize 3 x1 + 2 x2 subject to x1 + x2 ≤ 4 and x1 + 3 x2 ≤ 6, both nonnegative. We might expect the best mix to sit inside those limits; instead it lands on a corner where two bind. Drag a line and the region re-shapes.

My first cut of this figure re-solved on every pixel of the drag, and the region flickered like a bad weld. Letting it settle once the line stops moving fixed it; the arithmetic was never the slow part.

Section 02 · The formulation pipeline

From words to a tableau

The Mind stops pouring long enough to keep books. Beside every budget it writes a second figure, the amount still unspent, so no scrap of capacity goes uncounted. A limit written as an exact balance is easier to solve than a limit written as a warning.

The fab-bay mix stays maximize 3 x1 + 2 x2, but each at-most limit becomes an exact equation once a slack is added: x1 + x2 + s1 = 4 and x1 + 3 x2 + s2 = 6, where s1 and s2 hold the feedstock and press time still to spare. Those equalities are the first rows of the tableau.

Section 03 · The signature

Walking the corners

Corner Case, the survey drone, will not cut across the cargo hold. It hops corner to corner along the hull, and at each landing the tableau on the wall redraws itself to match the pose the drone just struck.

A third product, phase-crystal, enters as x3, so the program grows to maximize x1 + x2 + x3 under five resource limits, and its best corner sits at (9, 9, 4) with objective 22. Every pivot on that walk is Gaussian elimination on the tableau: the entering column and the leaving row that light up together are one step of row reduction.

Trust the picture only this far. In two or three dimensions you can see the corners; past three there is nothing left to see, the number of corners can climb faster than any screen could hold, and several corners can pile onto a single point, so the drone seems to pivot without moving. None of that reaches the tableau. The row reduction on the right is the same operation in three dimensions or three hundred, which is why the algebra, not the geometry, is what a solver actually runs.

If you stop here, you can already read a tableau: the corners of a region, walked one pivot at a time, with the objective climbing at each hop.

Section 04 · Duality

What one more unit is worth

At the best corner the Mind turns the question inside out. It stops asking how to fill the hold and asks instead what one more gram of each resource would buy if it could be had, and which resources are already worth nothing at the margin.

Each resource answers with its shadow price, its dual value: at this optimum one more chargrid hour is worth 1/7, one more unit of hull-frame stock is worth 3/7, and one more crystal-chamber slot is worth a full 1, while the antimatter budget and stabilizer mass, both with room to spare, are worth 0. The ship earns its name here: a shadow price is a marginal utility.

Stop here and you can already price a constraint: its shadow price, the dual value sitting on it, is exactly what one more unit of that resource is worth.

Section 05 · The playground

The worst case, built on purpose

Corner Case was built to visit corners. Someone, it seems, has now built the corners for it: this cargo manifold is skewed just enough that the drone's own greedy instinct drags it across every last vertex before the exit shows itself.

The manifold is maximize 100 x1 + 10 x2 + x3 under three tilted limits, and Dantzig's rule marches through all 2³ = 8 corners before it lands at (0, 0, 10000). Stretch the same construction to n = 200 and the tour runs to about 2 to the 200 pivots. The worst case is not rare, it is constructed.

Section 06 · Applications and the race

Networks and the race

The same drone that walked the cargo hold is loaned to the fleet, first to plot shuttle courses, then to balance relay traffic across a dozen ships. A sleeker vessel watches it work. Strictly Feasible has never once touched a wall, and it means to prove the long way round the boundary was never necessary.

Both jobs are linear programs wearing network clothes: read as node potentials the graph returns a shortest route of dt = 4, and read as arc capacities the same shape pushes a maximum flow of 8. The race then replays simplex along the boundary against the interior-point central path across one shared polytope, and interior points are strictly feasible points, which is how the rival earns its name.

Colophon

How this was checked, and where it comes from

Oracle-verified

Every fixture optimum on this page is cross-checked against scipy's HiGHS solver (scipy.optimize.linprog(method="highs"), which minimizes, so we negate the objective to maximize). The oracle badge links to exactly how that check runs, so no number here asks to be taken on faith.
