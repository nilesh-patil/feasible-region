Cold open

In 1939, George Stigler set out to find the cheapest diet that could keep a person alive and well: seventy-seven foods, no computer. His team ground through the arithmetic by hand, roughly 120 person-days of it, and landed on $39.93 a year. It was very nearly right. The method that could prove it would not arrive for another decade.

Ten thousand years later, that same mathematics has become plumbing. The General Systems Vehicle Marginal Utility provisions itself every few milliseconds: what to fabricate, what to spend, what to keep in reserve, against limits that shift by the second. It settles Stigler's question, and harder ones, before the thought finishes. This is a field guide to how.

Section 01 · The hero figure

A region with corners

Deep in the Marginal Utility, a fabricator bay splits its shift between field-tile and shear-foam. Two budgets bind it, feedstock and press time; neither can be crossed. The Mind wants the most valuable mix it can pour.

Underneath, two variables: maximize 3 x1 + 2 x2 subject to x1 + x2 ≤ 4 and x1 + 3 x2 ≤ 6, both nonnegative. We might expect the best mix to sit inside those limits; instead it lands on a corner where two meet. Drag a line and the region re-shapes.

My first cut of this figure re-solved on every pixel of the drag, and the region flickered like a bad weld. Letting it settle once the line stops moving fixed it; the arithmetic was never the slow part.

Section 02 · The formulation pipeline

From words to a tableau

The Mind writes its budgets down in plain words, then does the thing a careful ledger clerk would: it gives the leftover a name. Every limit earns a second line for the room it still has to spare.

The brewery mix stays maximize 3 x1 + 2 x2, but each at-most limit turns into an exact equation once a slack is added: x1 + x2 + s1 = 4 and x1 + 3 x2 + s2 = 6, where s1 and s2 hold the spare capacity left on each resource. Those equalities are the first rows of the tableau.

What sits above is the still frame the trace draws. Milestone B animates the three stages under a prev and next control, and the box is already sized to their footprint so this paragraph never jumps.

Section 03 · The signature

Walking the corners

Corner Case, the survey drone, will not cut across the cargo hold. It hops corner to corner along the hull, and at each landing the tableau on the wall redraws itself to match the pose the drone just struck.

A third product, phase-crystal, enters as x3, so the program grows to maximize x1 + x2 + x3 under five resource limits, and its best corner sits at (9, 9, 4) with objective 22. Every pivot on that walk is Gaussian elimination on the tableau: the entering column and the leaving row that light up together are one step of row reduction.

Stepping the walk is live now. The deeper zoom that unfolds a single pivot into its full row reduction is the next thing to land in this frame.

If you stop here, you can already read a tableau: the corners of a region, walked one pivot at a time, with the objective climbing at each hop.

Section 04 · Duality

What one more unit is worth

The Mind runs the numbers backward. It stops asking how to fill the hold and asks instead what a single extra gram of any resource would buy, and whether that gram is worth requisitioning at all.

That worth is the constraint's shadow price. At this optimum one more unit of crystal chamber capacity lifts the best objective by exactly 1, its dual value, while a limit with room to spare, like the antimatter budget, is worth 0 more. The ship earns its name here: a shadow price is a marginal utility.

Milestone B makes this pair move together: drag a limit on the left and its shadow price on the right updates in step. The frame is blocked out at full size now, which is why nothing here shifts when it loads.

If you stop here, you can read a shadow price: the worth of one more unit of a resource is the dual value sitting on that constraint.

Section 05 · The playground

The worst case, built on purpose

Somebody built this cargo manifold to be cruel. Its walls are skewed just enough that Corner Case, following its usual greedy instinct, is lured into touching every last corner before it stumbles on the exit.

The manifold is maximize 100 x1 + 10 x2 + x3 under three tilted limits, and Dantzig's rule marches through all 2³ = 8 corners before it lands at (0, 0, 10000). Stretch the same construction to n = 200 and the tour runs to about 2 to the 200 pivots. The worst case is not rare, it is constructed.

The place-your-bets slider and the reveal are milestone B work. For now the dotted frame just holds the seat, so the count you read above is the whole of this pass.

Section 06 · Applications and the race

Networks and the race

The same drone that walked the hold now plots shuttle courses and balances relay traffic across the fleet. Then a rival vessel, Strictly Feasible, lines up for a race it insists on running straight down the middle.

Both networks are linear programs in disguise: read as node potentials the graph gives a shortest route of dt = 4, read as capacities it pushes a maximum flow of 8. The race then replays simplex along the boundary against the interior-point central path across one shared polytope, and interior points are strictly feasible points, which is how the rival earns its name.

Both the two-card gallery and the race land in milestone B. Their frames are drawn at final size already, so the figures will slot in without nudging a single line of this text.

Colophon

How this was checked, and where it comes from

Oracle-verified

Every fixture optimum on this page is cross-checked against scipy's HiGHS solver (scipy.optimize.linprog(method="highs"), which minimizes, so we negate the objective to maximize). The oracle badge is a trust marker, not decoration: it links to exactly how the check runs, so no number here asks to be taken on faith.
